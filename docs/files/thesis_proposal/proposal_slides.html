A Drone in Mid-Flight

A drone in mid-flight may suddenly deviate from its intended trajectory, leaving only a narrow time window to diagnose the anomaly and apply corrective action before failure becomes unavoidable.

  • Inaccurate tracking, unaccounted disturbance, sensor degradation, actuator faults, adversarial interference

  • Detection and mitigation must occur under strict constraints

  • Quadrotors are underactuated, highly nonlinear, open-loop unstable; tight onboard compute budgets

  • Intersection of control theory, real-time hardware, and cybersecurity

This proposal is organized around addressing these challenges with computational efficiency at the forefront

The Practical–Research Gap

Despite major advances in aerial trajectory planning and control, state-of-the-art methods remain time- and compute-heavy — hard to deploy on real hardware.

  • Particularly pronounced on miniature aerial platforms, where onboard compute is often limited
  • NMPC is the state-of-the-art, yet: “impractical to run NMPC on some miniature aerial vehicles with a limited computational budget” [1]
  • LQR, differential-flatness, \(H_\infty\) alternatives are either computationally complex, or require advanced reference derivatives or detailed model knowledge

The practical state-of-the-art remains largely tethered to advanced PID control — a gap between the practical and research state-of-the-art.

Newton–Raphson Flow Controller

For \(\dot x = f(x,u),\; y = h(x)\), introduce a differentiable look-ahead predictor \(\hat y(t+T) = \rho(x(t), u(t))\) under zero-order hold on \(u\). Applying NRT to the predictor [2]:

\[ \Psi(x,u) \;\triangleq\; \Bigl(\tfrac{\partial \rho}{\partial u}(x,u)\Bigr)^{-1} \bigl(r(t+T) - \rho(x,u)\bigr), \qquad \dot u(t) = \alpha\,\bigl(\Psi(x,u) + \eta\bigr) \]

\[ u_{k+1} = u_k + \dot u(t_k)\Delta t \]

Asymptotic error bound: \[\limsup_{t\to\infty}\lVert r(t)-y(t)\rVert \leq \nu_1 + \frac{\nu_2}{\alpha}\]

\(\nu_1\) — prediction-model mismatch: gap between predictor \(\rho\) and true \(y(t+T)\); can’t be attenuated
\(\nu_2\) — reference-variation residual: scales with \(\sup_t\lVert\dot r(t+T)\rVert\); attenuated by speedup \(\alpha\)

Integral CBFs [3] \[\eta \;=\; \arg\min_{\eta}\tfrac12\lVert\eta\rVert^2 \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \dot b + \gamma(b) \ge 0\]

  • CBF for dynamic control laws
  • Barrier \(b(x,u)\) — safety in the state–input set, not just \(x\)
  • Admits QP even for non-control-affine systems

Prediction Strategies

The central design choice is the prediction strategy. Throughout our work, we’ve developed different approaches with various tradeoffs:

  1. Simplified closed-form linearized predictor. Small-angle approx; predictor and Jacobian computed offline
    • Pure matrix-vector costs
    • 100 Hz on RPi; no compilation needed
  2. Flatness-based predictor. Prediction is kinematic expansion in flat space
    • Bypass ODE integration; 100Hz without compilation
    • Fast computation; sacrifice accuracy
  3. Learned predictor. \(\rho_{\mathrm{NN}}(x,u;\theta)\)
    • Feedforward and RNN successful in simulation
    • Project won \(3^{rd}\) place in Deep Learning Symposium
  4. Nonlinear RK4 predictor. Compiled
    • Full-model RK4/Fwd Euler
      • JIT-Compilation w/forward-mode autodiff
      • Cython + finite-difference

\(\alpha\)-Stability for Miniature Blimp

Proposition [5]. For the hover linearization of the six-DOF miniature blimp with the exact ZOH predictor \(\hat y(t)=Ce^{AT}x + CA^{-1}(e^{AT}-I)Bu\), the closed-loop linearized system is \(\alpha\)-stable: \[\lim_{\alpha\to\infty}\limsup_{t\to\infty}\lVert r(t)-\hat{y}(t)\rVert=0.\]

Proof sketch. With extended state \(z \mathrel{:=} [x^\top\, u^\top]^\top\), NRT-flow yields the linear closed loop \[\dot z = \Phi_\alpha\, z + \Psi_\alpha\, r(t+T).\] Following [2], \(\alpha\)-stability reduces to two system polynomials \(P_0(s), Q(s)\) being Hurwitz. For the hover linearization both are \(\alpha\)-independent and have all roots in the LHP → sufficient conditions hold for every \(\alpha>0\). \(\blacksquare\)

Safe Planning Under Uncertainty

RTD Core [7]:

  • Planning model. Simple model used for FRS computation offline; worst-case tracking-error inflation absorbs model mismatch.
  • Offline FRS. Trajectories parameterized by \(k\).
  • Online Optimization. \(k^\star=\arg\min_{k\in K_{\mathrm{adm}}} J(k)\ \ \textit{s.t. } k \textit{ is obstacle-free}\)

Limitations we address:  (1) worst-case inflation → safe trajectories falsely deemed infeasible  ·  (2) offline FRS cannot absorb a priori unknown disturbances.

RTD-RAX Architecture

Departure: strip the offline FRS of its tracking-error inflation and delegate safety certification to the online MMR verifier against the measured disturbance. First RTD-class framework to safely plan under a priori unknown disturbances.

Mixed-Monotone Reachability Primer

For \(\dot x=f(x,u,w)\), \(x_0\in[\underline x_0,\overline x_0]\), \(u\in[\underline u,\overline u]\), \(w\in[\underline w,\overline w]\) [9]:

\(\mathsf F=[\underline{\mathsf F},\overline{\mathsf F}]\) inclusion function for \(f\): \(f(x,u,w)\in\mathsf F([\underline x,\overline x],[\underline u,\overline u],[\underline w,\overline w])\).

\(\mathsf F\) induces the \(2n\)-dim embedding system \(\mathsf E\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \dot{\underline x}_i &= \underline{\mathsf E}\!\bigl([\underline x,\overline x],[\underline u,\overline u],[\underline w,\overline w]\bigr)_i \;\mathrel{:=}\; \underline{\mathsf F}\!\bigl([\underline x,\overline x_{[i:\underline x]}],[\underline u,\overline u],[\underline w,\overline w]\bigr)_i,\\ \dot{\overline x}_i &= \overline{\mathsf E}\!\bigl([\underline x,\overline x],[\underline u,\overline u],[\underline w,\overline w]\bigr)_i \;\mathrel{:=}\; \overline{\mathsf F}\!\bigl([\underline x_{[i:\overline x]},\overline x],[\underline u,\overline u],[\underline w,\overline w]\bigr)_i. \end{aligned} \]

Monotonicity. The flow \(\Phi^{\mathsf E}\) of \(\mathsf E\) preserves the southeast order \(\preceq_{\text{SE}}\) on \(\mathbb R^{2n}\): tighter initial intervals and signals yield tighter embedding trajectories for all \(t\ge 0\).

Reachable-tube guarantee. For \(T\ge 0\), \(x_0\in[\underline x_0,\overline x_0]\), admissible \(w(\cdot)\), \[\phi(T;x_0,u,w)\in[\![\Phi^{\mathsf E}(T;[\underline x_0,\overline x_0],[\underline u,\overline u],[\underline w,\overline w])]\!]\]

MMR gives us hyperrectangular FRS over-approximation via a single \(2n\)-dim ODE integration.

immrax [6] constructs \(\mathsf F\) from the dynamics and JIT-compiles the embedding system for real-time reachability.

P2.1 — Tighter RTD/MMR Coupling

Current. RTD candidate-generation and online MMR verifier communicate only through accept/reject. Rejected candidates are discarded and re-optimized.

Proposed. Feed gradients of the online tube’s collision margin w.r.t. trajectory parameter \(k\) back into the RTD solver — gradient-based re-planning that directly optimizes certified safety margin.

\[ k_{\text{new}} \leftarrow k^\star + \eta\;\nabla_k\bigl(\min_i\operatorname{dist}(\mathcal B_j(k), \mathrm{obs}_i)\bigr) \]

P2.2 — Learned GP Disturbance Bounds

Current. \(w(t)\in[\underline w,\overline w]\) is a fixed hyper-rectangle.

Proposed. Learn a time-varying GP disturbance model from measured residuals during flight; map GP posterior mean + confidence region into time-varying interval bounds for the MMR verifier.

Reuses machinery from the Thrust 1 forward-invariant-tube work (planar quadrotor + GP wind fields).

P2.3 — Hardware Validation

  • Step 1: GTernal ground robot — port unicycle case studies, characterize real disturbances, validate repair online
  • Step 2: Holybro X500 quadrotor — higher-dimensional MMR; exploit differential-flatness structure to keep the embedding tractable

First hardware demo of disturbance-aware reachability-certified aerial planning.

P2.4 — RTD-RAX for STL Missions

Extend from spatial safety to full STL mission specifications by learning which control parameters map onto given STL formulas:

  • Offline: sample \((k, \varphi)\) pairs; train \(\hat\varphi = h_\theta(k)\)
  • Online: constrained search over \(\{k : h_\theta(k) \models \varphi\}\)
  • Safety certificate still from the MMR verifier

P2.5 — Learning-Accelerated STL (RL-STL)

STL-based control synthesis typically requires a mixed-integer program (MILP) — expensive to re-solve every step.

Pipeline

  1. Train an RL policy \(\pi_\theta\) offline to imitate MILP solutions over a distribution of environments and specifications
  2. At runtime: run \(\pi_\theta\) each step — fast policy inference
  3. Re-solve the MILP only when STL robustness \(\rho_\varphi\) drops below threshold \(\rho_{\text{trig}}\)

  1. Expected online compute dominated by policy inference.

  2. Safety maintained because MILP re-solve is triggered by any robustness degradation.

  3. Threshold \(\rho_{\text{trig}}\) exposes an explicit compute–conservatism tradeoff.

Thrust 3 — Overview

Like a biological immune response, accumulate certified safety-preserving strategies so each new attack expands the resilient operating envelope.

① Diagnose · NSF T1

Real-time root-cause isolation of the anomaly.

② Recover · NSF T2

Online recovery into a safe operating regime.

③ Inoculate · This Thesis

Reuse immrax MMR to certify backup controllers per fault class; NRT tracker executes the selected primitive.

P3.1 — Parameter → Safe-Strategy Map

For each fault class \(f\), precompute an offline map \[\sigma_f : \mathcal{P} \to \Sigma_f\] from system parameter \(p \in \mathcal{P}\) (altitude, speed, battery) to a pre-certified backup strategy \(\sigma \in \Sigma_f\).

  • Offline sweep: MMR-verify candidates on a grid of \((f, p)\)
  • Runtime: O(1) lookup → NRT tracker executes the selected strategy

Parallel to RTD-RAX P2.4. There: parameter \(\rightarrow\) STL-satisfying trajectory. Here: (fault, parameter) \(\rightarrow\) certified safe strategy.

  • Coverage guarantee: if \((f,p)\) is in the sampled grid, the strategy is MMR-certified by construction
  • Outside the grid: fall back to the online inoculation pipeline

P3.2 — STL Cached Failure-Mode Warm-Starts

Each fault class \(f\) carries an STL mission \(\varphi_f\) encoding its safe-recovery intent (return-to-home, avoid corridor, stabilize altitude).

Offline: solve the MILP for \(\varphi_f\) across the parameter grid, cache warm-starts \(z^*_f(p)\).

Runtime on trigger: retrieve \(z^*_f(p)\) → warm-start MILP → near-instant feasible recovery.

Reuses T2 RL-STL MILP machinery. Same encoding as P2.5; here the cache keys on the fault class, not a learned policy.

  • Cache hit: sub-second recovery
  • Cache miss: falls back to cold MILP — safety from the inoculation pipeline still holds
  • Ties parameter indexing (P3.1) and STL encoding (T2) into a single recovery layer

Backup — NRT Controller Derivation

Start from the classical scalar Newton-Raphson root-finder for \(f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) [5]: \[x_{k+1} = x_k - \frac{f(x_k)}{f'(x_k)}, \qquad\text{(vector form)}\;\; x_{k+1} = x_k - \Bigl(\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_k)\Bigr)^{-1} f(x_k).\]

Memoryless plant \(y(t)=g(u(t))\); seek \(u\) such that \(g(u)=r\). Discretize with step \(\Delta t\), \(t_k=k\Delta t\): \[u(t_{k+1}) \;=\; u(t_k) + \Delta t\,\Bigl(\tfrac{\partial g}{\partial u}(u(t_k))\Bigr)^{-1}\bigl(r(t_k)-g(u(t_k))\bigr).\]

Rearrange as a difference quotient and take the limit \(\Delta t\to 0\) to obtain the continuous-time NRT flow; scaling by a speedup factor \(\alpha>1\) yields [2]: \[\boxed{\;\dot u(t) = \alpha\,\Bigl(\tfrac{\partial g}{\partial u}(u(t))\Bigr)^{-1}\bigl(r(t)-g(u(t))\bigr)\;}\qquad\limsup_{t\to\infty}\|r(t)-y(t)\|\;\leq\;\alpha^{-1}\limsup_{t\to\infty}\|\dot r(t)\|.\]

For dynamical systems \(\dot x=f(x,u)\), \(y=h(x)\): replace \(y\) with a ZOH look-ahead predictor \(\hat y(t+T)=\rho(x(t),u(t))\). The NRT flow becomes \(\dot u=\alpha(\partial_u\rho)^{-1}(r(t+T)-\rho(x,u))\); asymptotic error bound tightens to \(\nu_1+\nu_2/\alpha\) (Cor. 3.5). \(\alpha\) is a speedup, not a gain — it multiplies \(\dot u\), not \(u\).

Backup — ZOH Predictor \(\rho(x,u)\): Construction

ZOH assumption [2]: hold \(u\) constant over \([t, t+T]\) and define \[ \rho(x(t),u(t)) \;\triangleq\; C\,x(t+T)\;\big|_{\dot x = f(x,u(t)),\; x(t)=x(t)}. \]

This is the (model-predicted) output at the lookahead horizon \(t+T\) assuming the current \(u\) stays frozen.

  • Linearized case (\(f(x,u)=Ax+Bu\)): closed-form \(\rho(x,u)=C e^{AT}x + C\!\int_0^T e^{As}\mathrm ds\,B\,u =: C\tilde A x + C\tilde B u\) → constant Jacobian \(J=C\tilde B\) [5]
  • Nonlinear case: \(\rho\) via numerical integration (RK4/Euler); \(\partial_u\rho\) via autodiff or finite differences [11]
  • Horizon \(T\) is a tuning knob: small \(T\) → high-frequency noise; large \(T\) → stale prediction

Residual bound \(\eta_1\): sup-norm of \(\|\rho(x,u)-y(t+T)\|\) across admissible \((x,u)\). Mismatch between predicted and true output is the only tracking-error floor \(\alpha\) cannot remove. This motivates the learned-predictor tier in Thrust 3.

Backup — \(\alpha\)-Stability: Definition

Definition 4.1 [2]. The NRT-closed-loop system is \(\alpha\)-stable on compact \(\Gamma\subset\mathbb R^{n+m}\) if \(\exists\,\bar\alpha\geq 0\) and class-\(\mathcal K\) functions \(\beta,\gamma_1,\gamma_2\) such that for every \(z(0)\in\Gamma\), every bounded reference \(r\) with bounded \(\dot r\), and every \(\alpha\geq\bar\alpha\), \[\lVert z(t)\rVert \leq \beta(\lVert z(0)\rVert) + \gamma_1(\lVert r\rVert_\infty) + \gamma_2(\lVert\dot r\rVert_\infty).\]

Intuition. The extended state \((x,u)\) stays bounded by three additive budgets: a transient from the initial condition, a tax for reference magnitude, and a tax for reference speed. Crucially, the bound holds for every \(\alpha\geq\bar\alpha\) with the same \(\beta,\gamma_1,\gamma_2\) — so cranking the learning gain tightens tracking (Cor.~3.5) without ever enlarging the BIBS envelope.

Key property: the bounds hold uniformly in \(\alpha\) once \(\alpha\geq\bar\alpha\) — increasing the gain never destabilises. Coupled with Corollary 3.5: \(\lim_{\alpha\to\infty}\limsup_t\lVert r-\hat y\rVert=0\). This is the BIBS-with-speedup variant; distinct from standard ISS in that \(\alpha\) appears as a control parameter, not a fixed gain [2].

Backup — \(\alpha\)-Stability: Closed-Loop

Extended state \(z=[x^\top,u^\top]^\top\); substituting the linear predictor into the NRT dynamics: \[\Phi_\alpha=\begin{bmatrix}A & B\\ -\alpha J(T)^{-1} Ce^{AT} & -\alpha I\end{bmatrix},\quad \Psi_\alpha=\begin{bmatrix}0\\ \alpha J(T)^{-1}\end{bmatrix}.\]

\(J(T)=CA^{-1}(e^{AT}-I)B\) when \(A\) is nonsingular; result holds even if \(A\) is singular provided \(J(T)\) is. Sufficient condition [2, Thm. 4.3]: the characteristic polynomial of \(\Phi_\alpha\) factors as \(\det(sI-\Phi_\alpha)=\alpha\,Q(s)+P_0(s)\) where \(P_0, Q\) depend only on \((A,B,C)\); \(\alpha\)-stability holds whenever both \(P_0(s)\) and \(Q(s)\) are Hurwitz.

Backup — Where \(\nu_1+\nu_2/\alpha\) Comes From

Corollary 3.5 of [2]. For any bounded reference with bounded derivative, the tracking error of the NRT flow satisfies \[\limsup_{t\to\infty}\lVert r(t)-y(t)\rVert \;\leq\; \eta_1 + \tfrac{\eta_2}{\alpha}.\]

\(\eta_1\) — sup-norm of the prediction-model residual \(\lVert\rho(x(t),u(t)) - y(t+T)\rVert\); vanishes only if \(\rho\) matches the true ZOH output exactly. Floor that \(\alpha\) cannot touch.

\(\eta_2\) — proportional to \(\sup_t\lVert\dot r(t+T)\rVert\), the reference’s own rate of change; attenuated by \(1/\alpha\) because larger \(\alpha\) tracks the moving target faster.

Operational consequence: increasing \(\alpha\) buys you diminishing returns toward \(\eta_1\); the plateau in the empirical \(\alpha\)-vs-RMSE sweep is \(\eta_1\) made visible.

Backup — iCBF Mechanism Sketch

The NRT dynamic law \(\dot u = \phi(x,u,t)\) is augmented with a min-norm perturbation \(v\in\mathbb R^m\) to enforce safety on the augmented set \(\mathcal S=\{(x,u)\in\mathbb R^{n+m}:h(x,u)\geq 0\}\) [3, Thm. 1]:

\[\dot u \;=\; \phi(x,u,t) + v^\star,\qquad v^\star \;=\; \operatorname{arg\,min}_{v\in\mathbb R^m}\;\tfrac{1}{2}\lVert v\rVert^2 \quad\text{s.t.}\quad p(x,u)^\top v \;\geq\; d(x,u,t),\]

with \(p(x,u)\!:=\!(\partial_u h)^\top\) and \(d(x,u,t)\!:=\!-\bigl[\partial_x h\,f(x,u)+\partial_u h\,\phi(x,u,t)+\gamma(h(x,u))\bigr]\).

  • I-CBF condition [3, Def. 1]: \(p(x,u)=0 \Rightarrow d(x,u,t)\leq 0\) — qualifies \(h\) as an I-CBF and guarantees QP feasibility
  • Single affine inequality + quadratic cost → min-norm closed form \(v^\star = \bigl(d/\lVert p\rVert^2\bigr)\,p\) if \(d>0\), else \(0\) [3, Eq. 19]
  • Holds even when \(f(x,u)\) is not control-affine — the paper’s core extension over classical CBFs

Input bounds are I-CBFs too: \(h_u(u)=u_{\max}^2-\lVert u\rVert^2\) satisfies the I-CBF condition [3, Lem. 1], so \(u\) is kept in-envelope by integration of \(\dot u=\phi+v^\star\) rather than by hard saturation — no windup. On the blimp, \(h\) combines thrust and tilt limits in \((x,u)\in\mathbb R^{16}\) [5].

Backup — Blimp Dynamics & Hover Linearization

Nonlinear model [5, Eq. (1)]: state \(x=(\nu^{b}_{b/n},\eta^{n}_{b/n})\in\mathbb R^{12}\) with \(\nu\) = body-frame translational/angular velocities, \(\eta\) = world-frame position + Euler angles. Input \(u=[f_x,f_y,f_z,\tau_z]^\top\in\mathbb R^4\), output \(y=[p_x,p_y,p_z,\psi]^\top=Cx\in\mathbb R^4\). Dynamics derived from submarine physics model: \[\dot x = \tilde A(x)x - (M^{CB})^{-1}G^{CB}(x) + (M^{CB})^{-1}u\] with mass/inertia \(M^{CB}\), Coriolis \(C^{CB}(x)\), damping \(D^{CB}\), and restoring gravity term \(G^{CB}(x)\) (buoyancy cancels gravity, but CG\(\neq\)CB induces nonzero roll/pitch torque) [5].

Hover linearization: about \(\nu=0\), \(\eta=0\), the nonlinear dynamics reduce to \(\dot x=Ax+Bu\) with constant \((A,B)\). Used as the NRT predictor on the blimp because constant Jacobian \(C\tilde B\) avoids any runtime inverse solve → 0.84 ms per control step on a Raspberry Pi Zero W [5].

Backup — Quadrotor Dynamic Model

9-state, 4-input nonlinear model used for the Holybro X500 V2 hardware [5]. State \(x=[p_x,p_y,p_z,\;V_x,V_y,V_z,\;\phi,\theta,\psi]^\top\in\mathbb R^9\); input \(u=[u_\tau,\;u_p,u_q,u_r]^\top\in\mathbb R^4\) (net thrust + body rates).

\[ \begin{aligned} (\dot p_x,\dot p_y,\dot p_z) &= (V_x,V_y,V_z), \\ \dot V_x &= -\tfrac{u_\tau}{m}\bigl(\sin\phi\sin\psi + \cos\phi\cos\psi\sin\theta\bigr), \\ \dot V_y &= -\tfrac{u_\tau}{m}\bigl(\cos\phi\sin\psi\sin\theta - \cos\psi\sin\phi\bigr), \\ \dot V_z &= g - \tfrac{u_\tau}{m}\bigl(\cos\phi\cos\theta\bigr), \\ (\dot\phi,\dot\theta,\dot\psi) &= T_\Theta\,\omega^b, \end{aligned} \]

where \(T_\Theta\) is the Euler-rate transformation matrix.

  • Tracked output \(y=(p_x,p_y,p_z,\psi)^\top\in\mathbb R^4\) — input/output dimensions match (\(m{=}p{=}4\)), so \(\partial_u\rho\) is square [5]
  • NRT predictor uses RK4 integration of the above from \((x(t),u(t))\) held constant over \([t, t+T]\); \(\partial_u\rho\) via JAX forward-mode autodiff
  • Body-rate commands feed the PX4 cascaded autopilot (stiff inner loop) → lookahead \(T\) chosen near its settling time

The quadrotor is differentially flat in \((p,\psi)\) but the flatness inverse explodes near hover attitude gimbal singularities [1]; NRT avoids this because it works directly with \(y=Cx\) on the closed-loop (PX4-PID-inside-the-loop) dynamics rather than requiring a flatness chart.

Backup — NMPC Baseline Formulation

Fixed-horizon optimal-control problem solved at each control step by acados’ SQP-RTI scheme [5]: \[ \min_{u_{0:N-1}}\;\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\bigl(\|y_k-r_k\|_Q^2 + \|u_k-u_k^{\mathrm{hover}}\|_R^2\bigr) + \|y_N-r_N\|_{Q_N}^2 \qquad\text{s.t.}\quad x_{k+1}=F_d(x_k,u_k),\;u_k\in\mathcal U. \]

  • \(F_d\) = 4th-order Runge–Kutta discretisation of the same nonlinear model used by the NRT predictor → apples-to-apples comparison
  • Weights tuned to give best achievable tracking within the 25 ms (blimp) / 10 ms (quad) budget
  • Delay compensation: solve from a forward-projected state \(\hat x(t+\tau_{\mathrm{comp}})\) to offset computation time
  • NMPC requires \((r_k, \dot r_k, \ddot r_k)\) over the horizon; NRT requires only \(r(t+T)\) [5]

NMPC wins in the absence of deadline pressure (e.g., X500 “clipped” RMSE, Table in Backup — Quad). It loses whenever the solve-time distribution has a heavy tail (blimp 60–73 ms vs. 25 ms budget; X500 with hover-to-reference transient).

Backup — Feedback-Linearization Baseline (Blimp)

Cascaded FBL for the nonlinear blimp model [5]: solve for virtual input \(q\in\mathbb R^4\) from the output dynamics then invert the input map: \[ q = \ddot r + K_d(\dot r - \dot y) + K_p(r - y), \qquad u(x) = \mathcal B(x)^{-1}\bigl(q - a(x)\bigr), \] where \(\mathcal B(x)\) and \(a(x)\) are derived by differentiating \(y=Cx\) along \(\dot x = f(x,u)\) until \(u\) appears (relative degree 2 for the miniature blimp’s position channel).

  • Provides exact cancellation of the nominal dynamics under the assumed model → linear residual closed-loop with PD gains \((K_p, K_d)\)
  • Requires full reference derivatives \((\dot r, \ddot r)\) — same as NMPC, unlike NRT
  • Fails gracefully on standard trajectories but diverges on aggressive trajectories where \(\mathcal B(x)\) becomes ill-conditioned (see Circle C in the Blimp RMSE table)

FBL is the canonical baseline for “smart-but-brittle”: no optimisation, low per-iter cost ($$10 ms), but any model mismatch propagates directly through \(\mathcal B^{-1}\). NRT achieves similar throughput and better robustness because the NRT flow implicitly regularises inversion through the \(\alpha\) integrator.

Backup — Contraction-NN Certified Region

[10] jointly trains a policy \(\pi_\theta\) and neural Riemannian metric \(M_\theta(x)\succ 0\) certifying exponential convergence of the closed-loop to any reference \((x'(t),u'(t))\):

\[\text{Tracking law: } u = \pi_\theta(x) - \pi_\theta(x') + u', \qquad\text{Contraction condition: } \dot M + \mathrm{sym}\bigl(M\,\partial_x f\bigr) \preceq -2\lambda M.\]

Verification via immrax: reformulate contraction LMI using an asymmetric matrix amenable to bound propagation → verified over the state region by \(2^n\) GPU-parallelized corner checks.

  • Certified region = training-time region; outside it, no formal guarantee
  • Both \(\pi_\theta\) and \(M_\theta\) are NNs; online cost = 2 forward passes

Backup — MMR Embedding Intuition

For \(\dot x=f(x,u,w)\) with \(x\in[\underline x,\overline x]\), \(u\in[\underline u,\overline u]\), \(w\in[\underline w,\overline w]\), a decomposition function \(\mathcal F:\mathbb R^n\times\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\) satisfies \(\mathcal F(x,x,\cdot,\cdot,\cdot,\cdot)=f(x,\cdot,\cdot)\) and is monotonically nondecreasing in its first \(n\) arguments, nonincreasing in the next \(n\) [9, Def. 2]. Stacking yields the embedding system on \(\mathbb R^{2n}\): \[ \begin{bmatrix}\dot{\underline x}\\\dot{\overline x}\end{bmatrix}=\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}\mathcal F(\underline x,\overline x,\underline u,\overline u,\underline w,\overline w)\\\mathcal F(\overline x,\underline x,\overline u,\underline u,\overline w,\underline w)\end{bmatrix}}_{\text{monotone in the southeast order on }\mathbb R^{2n}} \]

  • Southeast order: \((\underline x_1,\overline x_1)\preceq_{SE}(\underline x_2,\overline x_2)\iff \underline x_1\leq \underline x_2 \;\wedge\; \overline x_1\geq \overline x_2\) [9]
  • Integrating the embedding once yields a rigorous hyper-rectangular over-approximation of the forward reachable set, even for nonlinear \(f\)
  • Natural inclusion \(\mathcal F(x^\downarrow,x^\uparrow,\ldots)=[f]([x^\downarrow,x^\uparrow],\ldots)\); mixed-Jacobian inclusion gives tighter bounds using \(\partial_x f\) sign pattern [6, 12]
  • immrax automates decomposition-function construction via JAX tracing + interval-matrix arithmetic [6]

Backup — Forward Reachable Set (FRS): Definition

Parameterised FRS [7]: for a family of trajectories indexed by \(k\in K\subset\mathbb R^{n_k}\) starting from \(x_0\in X_0\), \[ \mathrm{FRS} \;=\; \bigl\{(z, k) \in Z\times K : \exists\,x_0\in X_0,\; t\in[0,T],\; z = \pi(x(t; k, x_0))\bigr\} \] where \(\pi\) projects to the workspace (2-D position on the ground, 3-D for aerial).

  • Computed offline via sum-of-squares programming: find polynomial \(p(z,k)\) with \(p(z,k)\leq 1\) on FRS and \(p(z,k)>1\) off FRS
  • Online use: each sensed obstacle point \(z_{\mathrm{obs}}\) gives constraint \(p(z_{\mathrm{obs}}, k)\leq 1-\delta\) → forbid \(k\) values whose FRS touches the obstacle
  • Standard RTD inflates \(X_0\) and \(K\) by the worst-case tracking error to guarantee safety [7]

RTD-RAX insight [8]: the inflation bakes in the worst disturbance realisation at design time. RTD-RAX uses the non-inflated FRS for planning and defers the safety check to a runtime MMR tube conditioned on the actual measured disturbance bound — tighter in practice, identical guarantee.

Backup — Swept Interval Hulls

Problem: MMR integration gives interval bounds \([\underline x(t_k),\overline x(t_k)]\) at discrete times \(t_k = k\Delta t\). A naive check at \(\{t_k\}\) can miss collisions that happen between samples. Solution used by RTD-RAX [8]: construct the swept interval hull over \([t_k, t_{k+1}]\) from a one-sided bound on \(\dot{\underline x}, \dot{\overline x}\): \[ \mathrm{Hull}_k \;=\; \bigl[\,\min\{\underline x(t_k), \underline x(t_{k+1})\} - \rho\Delta t,\;\; \max\{\overline x(t_k), \overline x(t_{k+1})\} + \rho\Delta t\,\bigr] \] with \(\rho\) an a-priori Lipschitz constant of the embedding RHS (certified by the same immrax framework).

  • Certification guarantee: reachable set on \([t_k, t_{k+1}]\) is contained in \(\mathrm{Hull}_k\) (proof by continuity + Lipschitz bound)
  • Cost: constant per step (one max/min + one vector add)
  • Conservatism: \(O(\Delta t)\) — tightens with smaller time step; step chosen to keep total FRS-inflation comparable to Standard RTD

Without swept hulls, RTD-RAX would need an expensive inter-sample check (e.g., interval Taylor, verified ODE integration) at every \(k\) — killing real-time performance. The hull trick is the engineering step that makes the online MMR certificate tractable inside the 100 ms planning budget.

Backup — RTD Online Optimization

Each candidate \(k\in K\) parameterises a cruise-then-fail-safe trajectory of length \(T=t_{\mathrm{plan}}+t_{\mathrm{stop}}\) [7]. Sensed obstacle points \(z_{\mathrm{FRS}}^{(i)}\) are mapped into FRS coordinates; each defines a scalar constraint \(q_i(k)=p(z_{\mathrm{FRS}}^{(i)},k)-1\) (polynomial in \(k\)). The planner solves \[\min_{k\in K_{\mathrm{adm}}}\; J(k) \quad\text{s.t.}\quad q_i(k)\leq 0,\;\; i=1,\dots,N_{\mathrm{obs}}.\]

  • \(J(k)\) drives the mission objective ( go toward goal)
  • \(K_{\mathrm{adm}}\subseteq K\) = admissible (kinematically feasible) parameters
  • Constraints and objective are smooth in \(k\) ⇒ analytic gradients, solvable by standard NLP

Standard RTD: inflated FRS itself is the safety certificate — no execution-time check needed. RTD-RAX: non-inflated FRS generates the candidate; the MMR tube provides the execution-time certificate instead.

Backup — RTD-RAX Repair, Formally

Given a rejected candidate \(k^\star\) (with collision index \(j^\star\) from the MMR online check) [8]:

  1. Speed back-off: \(k_2 \leftarrow \zeta_v\, k_2^\star\) with \(\zeta_v\in(0,1)\) → shorter trajectory time, tube grows less under uncertainty
  2. Lateral push: shift \(k_1\) orthogonal to the obstacle normal at the counter-example; reuse the same neighbourhood of the rejected \(k^\star\)
  3. Buffer tightening (CEGIS-style): inflate the obstacle buffer \(\delta_{j^\star}\) locally and re-solve the NLP over the non-inflated FRS
  4. Verify each repair candidate by re-integrating the MMR embedding under the measured disturbance bound \([\underline w,\overline w]\); execute the first candidate whose tube stays collision-free over \([t,t+T]\)

Fail-safe: if no repair candidate certifies within the 100 ms planning budget, the previously executed plan’s fail-safe braking segment is committed (guaranteed safe by construction of the RTD family). Cost per cycle (Case 3, seed 32): mean \(10.5\) ms (matches Std RTD); p95 \(37.4\) ms (vs. \(21.9\) Std) — growth is entirely in the tail when repair fires [8].

Backup — GP Mixed-Jacobian Inclusion Function

For \(\dot x = f(x) + B(x)\,g(t,x)\) with \(g\sim\mathcal{GP}(\mu(t,x), k((t,x),(t',x')))\), the Cao et al. 2026 construction [12] builds a decomposition function \(\mathcal F\) as follows:

Step 1 — Interval posterior. For any \([\underline x,\overline x]\), the GP posterior mean \(\hat g\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) over the box admit interval bounds \([\underline g, \overline g]\) from a confidence level \(\beta\): \(\underline g = \hat g - \beta\sigma,\;\overline g=\hat g+\beta\sigma\).

Step 2 — Mixed-Jacobian inclusion. Sign the entries of \(\partial_x f\) over the box; decompose \(f(x) = f^+(x) + f^-(x)\) where \(f^+\) has nonneg, \(f^-\) nonpos Jacobian. Then: \[ \underline{\mathcal F}(\underline x, \overline x, [\underline g,\overline g]) = f^+(\underline x) + f^-(\overline x) + B(\underline x)^+\underline g + B(\underline x)^-\overline g \] (upper analogously), yielding a tighter embedding than natural inclusion when \(\partial_x f\) has sign-stable entries.

  • Key insight: the mixed-Jacobian decomposition uses local sign information that natural inclusion ignores → often \(2\)–\(5\times\) tighter
  • Provably sound under the GP confidence level — \((1-\alpha)\)-probabilistic containment of the true reachable set
  • immrax extracts the sign pattern automatically via JAX tracing [6]

Without this step, the tube would have to use natural inclusion on the entire closed-loop dynamics — too conservative to fit inside the \(\varepsilon\)-tube for agile trajectories. Mixed-Jacobian inclusion is what makes the \(\varepsilon\)-trigger practical at gale-force wind speeds.

Backup — GP-Tube Runtime Trigger

For \(\dot x=f(x,u,w)\) with \(w\!=\!g(t,x)\) unknown, [12] build a forward-invariant tube of radius \(\varepsilon\) around the reference \(x_r(t)\) by (i) modelling \(g\) as a time-varying Gaussian process, (ii) using mixed-Jacobian inclusion to construct an embedding system, (iii) propagating \([\underline x(t),\overline x(t)]\) alongside \(x_r\) under the tracking controller.

Mechanism: integrate the embedding until the earliest time \(t^\star\) at which the tube can no longer stay inside the \(\varepsilon\)-neighborhood of \(x_r\). At \(t^\star\), trigger:

  1. Collect a fresh \(g\) sample
  2. Update the GP posterior
  3. Recompute reference + tube

Why this is sample-efficient: samples are only drawn when the certificate is about to expire — not on a fixed schedule. SITL: full pipeline in ~0.11 ms mean, tube-recompute at 100 Hz body-rate loop without missing deadlines.

Backup — STL Robustness & MILP Encoding

STL robustness \(\rho^\varphi(\mathbf x, t)\) quantifies how much margin a trajectory \(\mathbf x\) has to satisfying \(\varphi\) at time \(t\) [4]:

\[\rho^\mu(\mathbf x,t)=\mu(x_t),\quad \rho^{\varphi\wedge\psi}=\min(\rho^\varphi,\rho^\psi),\quad \rho^{\square_{[a,b]}\varphi}=\min_{t'\in[t+a,t+b]}\rho^\varphi(\mathbf x,t'),\quad \rho^{\lozenge_{[a,b]}\varphi}=\max_{t'\in[t+a,t+b]}\rho^\varphi(\mathbf x,t').\]

Big-M MILP encoding (Raman–Donzé style): introduce binary \(z_t\in\{0,1\}\) and slack \(\xi_t\) per predicate; encode \(\min\)/\(\max\) as:

\[\rho_t \leq M(1-z_t)+\rho_t^{(1)}, \quad \rho_t \leq M\,z_t + \rho_t^{(2)}, \quad z_t\in\{0,1\}.\]

Each \(\square\)/\(\lozenge\) adds \(O(\|\varphi\|)\) binary variables per time step.

RL-STL idea: run policy \(\pi_\theta\) (2 ms) instead of MILP (tens of ms). Only re-solve MILP when \(\rho^\varphi\) drops below threshold \(\rho_{\mathrm{trig}}\) — exposes explicit compute–conservatism tradeoff.

Backup — I-STL Semantics

Interval Signal Temporal Logic [4] lifts STL to interval-valued signals \(\mathbf X_t = [\underline x_t, \overline x_t]\): \[ \rho^\mu_{\mathrm I}(\mathbf X, t) \;=\; \bigl[\,\inf_{x\in\mathbf X_t}\mu(x),\; \sup_{x\in\mathbf X_t}\mu(x)\,\bigr], \qquad \rho^{\varphi\wedge\psi}_{\mathrm I} \;=\; \min_{\mathrm I}(\rho^\varphi_{\mathrm I}, \rho^\psi_{\mathrm I}). \]

  • \(\min_{\mathrm I}, \max_{\mathrm I}\) are the interval min/max operators (preserve interval soundness)
  • Soundness: if \(\underline\rho^\varphi_{\mathrm I}(\mathbf X) > 0\), every \(x\in\mathbf X\) satisfies \(\varphi\) ⇒ robust satisfaction under interval disturbance
  • Works with MMR tubes directly: feed \(\mathbf X_t = [\underline x(t), \overline x(t)]\) into I-STL semantics and check \(\underline\rho^\varphi_{\mathrm I} > 0\)

Why intervals, not scalar robustness? Scalar STL robustness \(\rho^\varphi\) is defined for a single trajectory; under disturbance, there’s no single trajectory, only a reachable set. I-STL is the language-level operator that certifies the set.

Backup — I-STL Persistently Feasible Set

Problem [4]: enforce I-STL formula \(\varphi\) for linear discrete-time system \(x_{k+1}=Ax_k+Bu_k+w_k\) with \(w_k\in\mathcal W\) bounded, under receding-horizon MIP. If the next-step MIP is infeasible, the previous backup sequence must still keep the system safe — otherwise the RTA is unsound.

Persistently feasible set \(\Omega_\varphi\): the set of states from which, under any disturbance realisation, there exists a control sequence satisfying \(\varphi\) over the remaining horizon. Built by backward reachability in the language space of \(\varphi\) using I-STL interval arithmetic.

Adding the terminal constraint \(x_{N}\in\Omega_\varphi\) to each MIP instance guarantees recursive feasibility for all time.

Deadline miss is safe: if the solver overruns, the previously computed backup trajectory is executed from the current state. Persistent feasibility guarantees the backup still satisfies \(\varphi\) under worst-case disturbance, without needing a new MIP solve.

Solved at 2 Hz in the blimp experiments; polynomial-spline interpolation + NRT LLC at 50 Hz.

Backup — Runtime-Assurance Switching Logic

Two-layer switching architecture [4, 5]:

\[ u(t) = \begin{cases} u_{\mathrm{perf}}(x(t), r(t)) & \text{if } \underline\rho^\varphi_{\mathrm I}(\mathbf X_{[t, t+H]}) > \rho_{\mathrm{trig}} \\ u_{\mathrm{safe}}(x(t)) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

  • \(u_{\mathrm{perf}}\) = performance controller (NRT flow, learned policy, NMPC, …) — any controller can be the performance layer
  • \(u_{\mathrm{safe}}\) = certified backup (I-STL MIP solution or library \(u_{b,j}\) from Inoculation slide)
  • Monitor: forward-propagate MMR tube \(\mathbf X_{[t, t+H]}\) at each step; evaluate I-STL robustness on the tube
  • Switch is one-way? No — return to \(u_{\mathrm{perf}}\) once \(\underline\rho^\varphi_{\mathrm I}\) recovers above \(\rho_{\mathrm{trig}}\) (no lock-in)

Hysteresis: use two thresholds \(\rho_{\mathrm{trig}} < \rho_{\mathrm{restore}}\) to prevent chattering; \(\rho_{\mathrm{restore}}\) is chosen from the certified region margin.

Preliminary I-STL RTA uses this architecture on the blimp at 2 Hz (monitor) / 50 Hz (NRT) [4].

Backup — Inoculation, Formally

Proposed Thrust 3 synthesis — extends the preliminary I-STL RTA [4] to post-fault recovery under cyber threat [13].

Under fault class \(\mathcal F_j\) identified (by NSF collaborator) via STL specification \(\varphi_j\):

  • Viability kernel \(\mathcal V_j = \{x_0 : \exists u(\cdot),\;x(t)\in\mathcal X_{\mathrm{safe}}\;\forall t\geq 0\}\) for post-fault dynamics is generally intractable online
  • Construct parameterized backup controller \(u_{b,j}\) whose certified operating domain \(\mathcal S_j \subseteq \mathcal V_j\) is forward-invariant under post-fault dynamics
  • Certification: propagate MMR tube [6, 9] under \(u_{b,j}\) starting from \(\mathcal S_j\); if \([\underline x(t),\overline x(t)]\subseteq\mathcal X_{\mathrm{safe}}\) for \(t\in[0,T_{\mathrm{rec}}]\), add \((u_{b,j}, \mathcal S_j, \varphi_j)\) to library
  • Online matching: locality-sensitive hashing of fault signatures → constant-time lookup of matching \(u_{b,j}\); fallback to RTA tube during search
  • Contract with NSF collaborators: they deliver \((\hat{\mathcal F}_j, \hat\varphi_j)\); we deliver the certified pair \((u_{b,j}, \mathcal S_j)\)

Backup — RL-STL Training Pipeline

Proposed Thrust 3 learned-RTA. Extends the I-STL MILP RTA [4] with a learned amortised policy.

Architecture:

  1. Offline data generation: sample states, disturbance patterns, and STL specifications; solve MILP RTA to get optimal \(u^\star(x, w, \varphi)\) trajectories
  2. Policy training: imitation-learn \(\pi_\theta(x, \varphi) \to u\) on the dataset; augment with STL-robustness reward \(\max \rho^\varphi\) via policy-gradient refinement
  3. Runtime switch: evaluate \(\pi_\theta\) at 50 Hz; compute I-STL robustness \(\underline\rho^\varphi_{\mathrm I}(\mathbf X_t)\) against a measured disturbance interval
  4. Trigger condition: if \(\underline\rho^\varphi_{\mathrm I} < \rho_{\mathrm{trig}}\) → re-solve MILP (falling back to I-STL persistent-feasibility guarantee [4]) to avoid unsafe drift
  • Why imitation from MILP? Gives correct-by-construction optimality target; avoids reward-shaping pitfalls of pure RL
  • Formal guarantee: persistent-feasibility set \(\Omega_\varphi\) of the MILP is the worst-case safety net; \(\pi_\theta\)’s failures are bounded by one MILP fallback
  • Compute target: \(\pi_\theta\) inference $$2 ms; MILP fallback $$20–50 ms (still sub-sample-time at 2 Hz planning)

Evaluation: number of MILP triggers vs. fixed-rate MILP (target: $$5× reduction); end-to-end STL satisfaction rate under worst-case disturbance (target: \(\geq\) MILP-only baseline).
